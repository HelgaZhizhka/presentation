Understanding Agile Methodology

Today we're going to explore Agile methodology, a game-changer in the field of product development and project management. Agile is known as a more flexible alternative to traditional Waterfall model, that often can be rigid in the face of change.

To understand why Agile is revolutionary, it's important to look at the Waterfall model first. Waterfall is a linear approach to project management where each phase is completed before moving on to the next. Although this method gives a structured way of organizing work, it hardly adapts to changes, what causes delays in product delivery.

What is Agile?

Agile is a way of thinking expressed in a set of values and principles. Requirements, plans, and results are continuously so teams have a natural mechanism for responding to changes quickly. The Agile approach creates a flexible working environment.

The Agile Manifesto

The Agile Manifesto is a document that sets out four key values and twelve principles of Agile. It was written in 2001 by seventeen software developers to sign a convenient set of rules for users.

The Agile Manifesto

Individuals and interactions over processes and tools

This lines up the importance of people and their interactions in achieving successful results. While processes and tools are important, they should not overshadow the human aspect of work. It's people who create, innovate, and adapt to change, not the tools they use.

Working software over detailed documentation

While documentation is important for reference and knowledge sharing, the main objective is delivering a working product. In Agile, teams focus more on solving problems and meeting user needs than on writing detailed documentation.

Customer collaboration over contract negotiation

Agile is more about regular feedback and open dialogue with customers rather than sticking strictly to terms. This approach allows for better understanding, meeting the customer's needs and adapting to changes quickly.

Agile Principles

Agile also includes 12 principles, which give further definition to the values

  • Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software. This means we should always aim to make the customer happy. We can do this by regularly giving them useful software.
  • Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. This means that it's okay if the customer changes their mind about what they want, even if we're almost done with the project. Changes can actually help the customer, so we should welcome them.
  • Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment, trust them and support their needs. This means we should let excited and driven people lead our projects. We should help them and trust them to do a great job.
  • At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then behave accordingly. This means that we should regularly take some time to think about how we can work better. Then, we should make changes based on what we know.

How to use Agile?

There are several frameworks within the Agile methodology, including Scrum and Kanban. Scrum is a highly structured framework that breaks down complex projects into manageable 'sprints'. In contrast, Kanban emphasizes continuous improvement, and teams use Kanban board to visualize their work. Agile methodologies also provide teams with flexibility to adapt to changes and help reduce the overall risk of project failure.

Challenges in using agile

However, using Agile can be challenging. Resistance to change, a lack of understanding the principles, and an absence of experienced leaders are common obstacles. But with the right mindset, desire to learning, and team collaboration, these challenges can be overcome.
